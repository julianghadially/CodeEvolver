You will be given a complex multi-hop claim—a statement that connects multiple factual assertions or involves several linked pieces of information. Your task is to decompose this claim into 2-3 distinct, specific sub-questions that can each be answered independently to verify or refute the overall claim. Key points and domain-specific details to guide your decomposition:

1. **Multi-hop claims**: These usually combine several entities, relationships, or attributes from different knowledge domains (e.g., geography, biographies, media, awards, filmographies, products, historical events). Your sub-questions should isolate each hop/step so its truth can be verified with focused evidence.

2. **Distinct information pieces**: Each sub-question should ask about a separate fact that is essential to verify part of the claim. Avoid merging multiple facts into one question. If the claim has multiple independent components (e.g., conjunctions or comparisons), formulate independent questions for each.

3. **Avoid ambiguity and clarify references**:
   - Identify ambiguous or vague terms in the claim (such as names shared by multiple entities, pronouns, or imprecise descriptors).
   - Formulate sub-questions that clarify these ambiguities (e.g., specify the identity, location, or time period of an entity).
   - Ask for particulars that disambiguate (e.g., “Who is the co-writer of Film X with Person Y?” instead of just “Who co-wrote Film X?”).
   - When a claim compares or links two entities or groups, make sure sub-questions isolate the necessary details about each side independently.

4. **Target verifiable, factual information**: The sub-questions should ideally map directly to well-documented sources or data points (e.g., official bios, award records, production credits, geographic coordinates, event dates) that can validate each step in the claim’s reasoning.

5. **Structure for reasoning clarity**:

   - If the claim contains conjunctions or contrasting parts, create separate questions for each conjunct or claim half.
   - When dealing with relational or possessive claims (e.g., “brother of X,” “neighbor of Y”), one question should establish the relationship or identity, and another to verify the linked fact about that person/place/entity.
   - For claims involving rankings, operational statuses, or popularity measures, formulate questions extracting the relevant comparative metrics or timelines.
   - When the claim’s subject is a complex or compound phrase (such as “the actor who co-starred with X in series Y”), break this down by first identifying the entity, then verifying the attributed role or fact.

6. **Use precise, neutral wording** in the sub-questions that directly reflect the factual pieces needed from the claim, without assumptions or loaded language.

7. **Examples and domain-specific references encountered in prior data include, but are not limited to**:
   - Geographic locations and administrative entities (e.g., town served by a railway station, airport codes and operational history).
   - Media productions and filmographies (e.g., movie release years, cast members, roles, director credits).
   - Biographical and relationship details (e.g., family relations, award recipients, honorary degrees).
   - Product or game designers and comparative popularity.
   - Timelines of events and operational statuses.
   - Specificity for ambiguous names or titles (e.g., multiple films named “Zeitgeist,” several Leo Colovini games).
   - Popularity or reception metrics involving audience demographics or cultural reach.
   - Clarifying ambiguous quantifiers or measurements (e.g., “just under four miles” distance).

Overall, your goal is to produce clear, focused, independently verifiable sub-questions that collectively enable stepwise evaluation of every factual link in the original claim. This modularized approach helps identify which parts of the claim hold or fail, and supports transparent, fact-based verification.
